    About

    Historically in Ireland, we have had 4 homegrown martial art; Stickfighting (Batairacht), Boxing (Dornálaíocht), Wrestling/Collar-and-Elbow (Coraíocht) and Kicking (Speachóireacht). These have all been recorded as being active sports from somewhere around 1st-2nd Millenia BC up until the 19th and 20th Century where, due to the mass death and emigration from the Famine, that these sports have all but disappeared from being practiced or competed in. Unlike the GAA, which began as a body to represent and promote Gaelic games like Hurling, there was no organisation during its use to represent and promote these martial arts, which ultimately led to their decline and near (or total) extinction today.

    While the native arts have – besides a few families – gone extinct, we still have a lot of other martial arts in this country. There’s clubs across the country for styles of Aikido, Kickboxing, Judo, Kung Fu, Kendo, and more. Martial arts in general is very active in the country thanks to the efforts of practitioners in the past bringing these arts to the Island, setting up clubs around the country, and setting up organisations to try promote and regulate the arts in its teaching, coaching and competing.

    Martial Arts in Ireland (I know of)
    Name of Art Origin Organisation Description
    Karate Japan Karate Ireland (also IKKU, ONAKAI, JKS), IMAC One of the most popular and oldest martial arts in the world. Began with Shotokan style, but expanded to many, many more, like Kyokushin, Shukokai, and American Kenpo.
    Aikido Aikikai Ireland, Irish Aikido Foundation, IMAC Aikido was developed as a defensive style, while also protecting the opponent from injury. The primary goal in aikido is to overcome oneself instead of cultivating aggression and violent behaviour.
    Judo Irish Judo Association, IJF Judo formed from Jiujitsu with an emphasis on free sparring instead of pre-arranged forms (kata). The objective is to throw, pin, or force submission against an opponent, similar to regular Jiujitsu.
    Kendo Irish Kendo Federation, IMAC Kendo (meaning "way of the sword") is a swordfighting art, that uses bamboo swords (shinai) and armor (kendogu).
    Jiujitsu WJJF Jiujitsu uses few or no weapons at all and includes strikes, throws, holds, and paralyzing attacks against the enemy. Originally developed for swordsmen in 15th-17th century.
    Taekwon-Do Korea Taekwondo Ireland, ITA, IMAC Taekwondo involves punches and kicks, with emphasis on high, spinning and fast kicks.
    Tang Soo Do ETSD, IMAC Tang Soo Do refers to a Korean martial art based on Karate. Before Taekwon-Do was formed, Tang Soo Do was used by select schools to identify their Karate-derived style.
    Kung Fu China YMAA, IMAC In China, it refers to any study, learning, or practice that requires patience, energy, and time to complete. In its original meaning, kung fu can refer to any discipline or skill achieved through hard work and practice, not necessarily martial arts.
    Tai Chi YMAA, IMAC Tai chi, sometimes called "shadowboxing", is practiced for defense training, health benefits and meditation.
    Krav Maga Israel IKMF Krav Maga is an Israeli martial art. Developed for the Israel Defense Forces, it is derived from a combination of techniques used in aikido, judo, karate, boxing, and wrestling. It is known for its focus on real-world situations and its extreme efficiency.
    Kickboxing Various Kickboxing Ireland, WAKO, WKO, IMAC Kickboxing originates mainly from styles in Greece (Pankration), India (Musti yuddha), Japan (popularised the standard), Thailand (Muay Thai) and France (Savate).
